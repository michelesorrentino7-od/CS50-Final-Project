The Landscape

CSRD asks you to report. CSDDD asks you to act. Both ask you to prove it.

CSRD

The Corporate Sustainability Reporting Directive is a disclosure obligation. It requires companies to report sustainability information — including value-chain impacts — under the ESRS standards, with double materiality as the entry point and limited assurance on the result.

Its reach extends well beyond the companies directly in scope: every in-scope reporter must collect data from its suppliers, which is why SMEs far below the thresholds are already receiving CSRD-driven questionnaires.

CSDDD

The Corporate Sustainability Due Diligence Directive is a conduct obligation. It requires companies to operate a due-diligence process: identify adverse human-rights and environmental impacts in their chain of activities, prevent or mitigate them, monitor effectiveness and provide remediation.

Reporting describes the process; the directive requires the process itself to exist and to work. That is an operating-model question — ownership, procedures, escalation — not a drafting exercise.

What Applies to You

Scope is the first question. Proportionality is the second.

Companies in or approaching CSRD scope

You need a readiness position you can defend: which ESRS topics are material, where the data gaps are, what evidence assurance will expect and a realistic plan to close the distance. A gap analysis only has value if it ends in an evidence plan with owners and dates.

SMEs inside someone else's value chain

Your enterprise customers are in scope, so their obligations arrive at your door as questionnaires, codes of conduct and contract clauses. You need a proportionate response: one set of reliable answers and evidence, reusable across every customer request, instead of improvising each time.

Organisations that depend on suppliers they don't control

Outsourced operations, logistics networks, subcontractor chains. Whether or not a directive formally applies to you, your name is attached to what happens there — and investors, lenders and customers increasingly ask how you know. You need a due-diligence model sized to your real exposure.

Supply Chain

The questionnaire is the easiest part. What happens after the answers come back is the due diligence.

Supply-Chain Due Diligence · Operating model

Most supplier due-diligence programmes stall at data collection: hundreds of questionnaires sent, a fraction returned, answers filed and never read again. Nothing in that exercise identifies a risk, mitigates an impact or survives a serious question from an auditor or customer.

A working model is narrower and deeper. Segment the supplier base so effort follows risk. Ask fewer questions and define what each answer changes. Classify, decide, document. Follow up on corrective actions until they close. Reassess when something material changes — a new country, a new subcontractor, an incident.

This is the same discipline as any third-party risk process — applied to human-rights and environmental exposure instead of only financial or operational risk. If you already audit your suppliers, due diligence extends that machinery; it does not require a parallel one.

If you already run supplier audits or third-party assurance, we build on it. If you are starting from zero, we size the model to your real exposure — not to the largest version of the requirement.
